Where it is

This is a ground-data-system architecture that would pass muster at a real mission ops shop, wrapped around a flight program that has barely left the runway — and I mean both halves precisely. The architecture half: an append-only event log as sole source of truth, every belief a deterministic re-runnable projection (D18: stable IDs, no wall-clock, evidence watermarks, thresholds as traced constants), decay measured in evidence-days against the corpus watermark rather than the clock, and replay defined as a projector called with a time bound instead of "latest." That is exactly how real mission telemetry systems work — you keep the raw downlink forever, you version the calibration, and any anomaly board must be able to reprocess the same data and get the same answer. D18 is genuinely load-bearing here, not decorative: qualification decay, the rot test, and replay-as-view all collapse without it. The self-review is also real: LEARNING-RATE-CRITIQUE reads like a competent independent review board — five named gaming vectors, a recommendation to ship a metric family instead of a blended scalar, an honest admission that three of Δ3's four terms need snapshot-diff machinery that does not exist. The replay doc independently converges on the same gap and says build it once. That convergence is systems engineering, not cosplay.

The flight-program half: the full corpus holds 16 observations across at least three workflows, but the 10 the association/coverage projections consume are single-workflow; findings sit at n=1–5 samples; zero associations, zero capabilities, zero experiment results in the live store; 18 stated coverage gaps (8 single-workflow-evidence + 10 unmeasured-metrics). Boxes, arrows, a legend I half-believed: "tracked and orchestrated with OTel and MS agent framework" — I wrote that the way you write "synergy" in a steering-committee deck, as a placeholder for competence I didn't have. Today my actual system is three terminals, agents stepping on each other's files, and an SSH script I'm embarrassed by. So I read these four documents from July the way I'd read a vendor's reference architecture: assume it's 60% aspiration until proven otherwise.

Here's what got me. The legend landed. The contracts directory holds ten schema files (the roadmap footer marks five shipped, with association and capability queued next — and those two shipped too); the wind-tunnel doc calls the OTel trace the organization's nervous system, and out on the fleet it's real — MAF middleware with OTel flowing into Jaeger. The throwaway annotation became the spine. And the yellow box I marked "hosted local, likely custom built" — my apology-in-advance for the SSH tape — got replaced with an MCP-first conductor, which is what I would have drawn if I'd known the word.

But I've watched enterprise architecture die in SharePoint for twenty years, and I know the smell of a program that got really good at describing itself. Three of these four documents are about things deliberately not built. The one capability the whole belief engine has derived is "we can run builds on Ollama," medium confidence, two workflows. And not one page — not one — has a customer's name on it. I drew "work creation" as the first box on the left edge for a reason. Let's do the map check honestly.

Hard truths
  1. Not one of these four documents has a customer's name in it. The organization that can state its own blind spots with four gap types has not stated the biggest one: nobody outside this house has paid it anything.
  2. Three of the four most polished artifacts describe things deliberately not built. I've written this genre for clients for twenty years — it dies in SharePoint unless a paying mission pulls it into existence. Restraint is only a virtue if the freed capacity went somewhere that pays.
  3. The belief engine passed 110/110 tests and has derived exactly one organizational belief: builds work on Ollama, medium confidence, two workflows. The philosophy-to-evidence ratio is inverted, and July-me knows it.

But I drew "work creation" at the left edge because arrows flow left to right, and right now the arrows start from inside the machine.
