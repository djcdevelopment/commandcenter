That "on-ramp" is probably worth treating as its own product.

Imagine bootstrapping a fresh machine:

Connect to MCP.
Discover available GPUs.
Discover available models.
Discover available agents.
Discover available workflows.
Verify credentials.
Run health checks.
Present capabilities.

After that, every model—whether it's a tiny local Haiku-equivalent, a GPT Mini, or a frontier model—has the same entry point into your ecosystem.

That's a much more resilient architecture than hard-coding assumptions about which machine is running what.

The pattern I see across everything you've built is that you're gradually replacing "tribal knowledge in your head" with executable systems. Every time you hit one of these friction points, you don't just fix the immediate problem—you end up designing a more general capability. That's exactly how MechNet seems to have evolved, and this feels like the next iteration: making your own infrastructure discoverable enough that even a modest local model can take over the operational work when the expensive reasoning models are unavailable.
