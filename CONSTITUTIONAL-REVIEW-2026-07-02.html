Constitutional Review — Two Economies / Wind Tunnel Deltas

Adversarial pass over Δ1–Δ5 (TWO-ECONOMIES-WIND-TUNNEL.html) against the constitution in CAPABILITY-ROADMAP.html. Guilty until proven derivable. This doc is the synthesis after cross-checking against the other four builder reviews (Δ3 detail in LEARNING-RATE-CRITIQUE.html, Δ5 detail in REPLAY-INFORMATION-ARCHITECTURE.html) — it captures the two constitutional amendments and the generalized invariant that came out of reconciling them, which none of the individual reviews wrote down.

Reviewer role: constitutional, not implementation Status date: 2026-07-02 (late) Verdict: no delta rejected outright; two need redesign, one metric needs splitting
Headline finding: none of the five reviews concluded the wind-tunnel idea was constitutionally flawed. All five converged on the same discipline: push interpreted state downward into observations and upward into projections, leaving as little authored state in the middle as possible.

Two amendments to the constitution

Amendment 1 — the residue clause generalizes

CAPABILITY-ROADMAP.html's Residue clause currently reads only "what to observe." The wind-tunnel doctrine adds a second authored object — the operating budget (how hot, how loud, how much wear the lab may spend unattended). Read side by side these look like a second exception. They are not: both are declarations of will, the same species as risk_accepted on an experiment plan. The clause should be rewritten to name the category, not enumerate its members one at a time:

"The irreducible authored residue consists only of declarations of will: what the organization chooses to observe, and what operating risk or budget it accepts. Everything else is projected from evidence."

This strengthens Clause 2 rather than weakening it — it closes the door on future additions being read as ad-hoc exceptions, as long as they're actually will and not fact.

Amendment 2 — the guardrail against new decision dimensions generalizes

The original objection to Δ1 was framed as a count: don't add a third _influence field alongside policy_influence / capability_influence. That framing doesn't scale and isn't the actual invariant — it was a smell-test standing in for a property that hadn't been named. The real law:

"Any new scheduler decision dimension must explain itself, be replayable, and be independently testable — the same obligations every existing decision dimension already carries. If a proposed dimension satisfies those three properties, its existence is not a constitutional problem. If it can't, it shouldn't exist regardless of how few dimensions currently exist."

This reclassifies the Δ1 problem: the defect isn't "too many influence fields," it's that cost_class: metered | sunk is currently a hand-assigned label with no stated evidence trail. The open question isn't "should this be a field," it's "is this authored-because-irreducible (like a resource-registration fact — no observation will ever tell you which node Derek physically owns) or authored-because-nobody-wrote-the-derivation-yet."

Cross-cutting pattern

Every finding across all five reviews reduces to the same move: when a proposed object looks like it might be authored truth, ask whether it can be pushed to one of the two legitimate poles — downward into a raw, timestamped observation (Δ2's residency fields, Δ4's idle boundary), or upward into a projection computed from existing derived objects (Δ3's ranking function, Δ1's cost_class if it turns out to be derivable). What's left in the middle after that push is the true test of Amendment 1: is it actually a declaration of will? If yes, it's constitutional residue. If no, it hasn't found its proper layer yet and shouldn't ship in the middle.
